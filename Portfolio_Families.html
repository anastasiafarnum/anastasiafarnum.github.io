Empathy For Families

THE PROCESS


Context:

Lack of accessibility is a pervasive issue that affects many aspects of life - including what should be fun! Common wayfinding tools and trip planning services do not consider the real world experience for multitasking parents with a baby on board (specifically in a stroller).

Opportunity:

Part user empathy exercise, part information architecture, and part product design, this project was a heavily prototyped and tested exercise where in the end I created a tool that would provide a day of accessible fun to a family carrying a baby with a stroller.

Outcome:

I created a physical map that hangs from different types of stroller handles to allow multitasking parents to easily receive the wayfinding information they need while navigating a specific area within Cambridge. The map also considers necessities such as the need for bathroom breaks and places to find snacks along the journey, aside from making sure everything is accessible to a stroller.

EXPERIENCING THE USER’S JOURNEY


Using A Stroller:

Through preliminary research, trying the designated route while pushing a stroller with a 30 lb sandbag, and shadowing users, the team and I were able to discover user pain points that occur throughout the journey, specifically when using a stroller. Some examples are shown below:



Traveling with a stroller creates fatigue for the person pushing it - so not only does the child need pit stops, but the parent could use breaks as well.


Doors are an obstacle! Automated doors are best when navigating around with a stroller.


Kids love parks - but grass and sand are an enemy to wheels! If smooth ground is nearby, it’s best to reroute to take that instead, even if the path is technically longer.



Lessons From Navigating:


A path may look fine when you first enter it, but obstacles may appear later. We observed an example of this when a sloped path near the galleria ended with a staircase.

Posted signs offer help with directions, but those suggestions do not have accessibility needs noted.

Beside signs, public art and other large environmental markers do well to help people discover where they are relative to where they want to be. Examples within East Cambridge are the Duck Tours, the totem statue near the galleria, and the overpass bridge.

PROTOTYPING AND USER TESTING



Building and testing the wayfinding tool was an iterative process that required user testing after each mock up. It’s not just about the map looking good, it needs to work well too - this means a parent with a stroller should find ease (and less obstacles) navigating the area while using the map.



With both hands needed to push the stroller, it became very apparent that a folded map would not work. Here's one of the prototypes of the hanging page design.


While pushing a stroller through an unfamiliar space and paying attention to a child, it’s important to avoid information overload on a map since there are enough distractions. This prototype has all the info, but the color scheme proved to be distracting when tested in the field.


The map is readable from the graphics alone, but if needed (if the parent has the time), supplementary text explanations are available on the backs of cards. There’s no need to flip the cards back and forth - the text on the back of the previous card corresponds with the current map facing the parent.

The Final Map:

Stroller Considerations:

+ The edges are rounded for safety

+ The map has a fun illustrative style that communicates information in a simple way

+ The physical map doubles as a toy, with similar features as current toys for babies

+ The plastic material is durable to drops and spills and also creates a satisfying clacking sound

+ The ring hangs from the stroller handle to keep hands free

Usability Considerations:

+  The estimated travel times are based upon our own trek with the baby carriage and were compared to Google’s estimate (which is based on a “regular” walking pace).

+  The design is color contrast checked for optimal visibility outdoors.

+  All the user testers prioritized using the visual map, so that is what will face the parent when hanging from the stroller.
