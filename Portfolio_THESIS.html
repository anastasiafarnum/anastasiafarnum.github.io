A Generation at Risk

a thesis project exploring how higher education can be reshaped for Gen Z

THE PROCESS


Context:

The current education system in America is outdated and based upon schooling methods from the past that reflected the industriousness needed to work in factories. Higher education has become a business with prices a student may pay through loans for years after leaving their institution. Job placement or even job satisfaction is not guaranteed after the time and money is spent. The thesis title is based off of the 1983 report titled ‘A Nation At Risk: The Imperative for Educational Reform’. That report was a call for reform in the education system in America which has still not been fully accomplished to this day.

Opportunity:

Gen Z has proved to be our most entrepreneurial generation as of yet. They are making waves in taking ownership of their futures by capitalizing on their talents before anyone else does, especially thanks to emerging technology. How can higher education adapt to the needs of Gen Z and how can Gen Z demand that colleges offer the quality that makes it worth the investment?

Outcome:

After user research, interviews, and meeting with stakeholders in the education space, I proposed that a workshop series for teenagers, lead by teenagers, can create a space to enable them to start the movement of education reform. The key elements to the workshop series would be talent and interest exploration through guest visits combined with a lot of asking “why?”. Having a teenager lead the sessions breaks down the power dynamics in place between teacher and student and instead encourages open and critical dialogue that reflects the Gen Z entrepreneurial spirit.

THE THESIS REPORT

The report documents the full thesis process which goes from research to prototyping solutions. Included below are the first 32 pages. The first semester of the project focused on background research, in-class observations, and user research. The second semester focused on researching and testing possible methods (i.e. games, programs, curricula) to accomplish the goal tied to the thesis topic: empowering students in high school to explore and take control of their educational future.
