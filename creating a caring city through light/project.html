    creating a focus

    about SAD:

    With consideration of light making an impact to emotional and physical well being, we decided to reference the condition SAD (Seasonal Affective Disorder) to inform our project. The fact that SAD is heavily influenced by environmental lighting, it gave us a specific case to design for, that would also affect those who relate to the symptoms of SAD.

    Seasonal Affective Disorder (SAD):

    4 out of 5 people who experience SAD in the U.S. are women. The main age of people experiencing SAD is between 20 and 30 years. Depression is estimated to cost the U.S. healthcare system $201 billion annually.

    Symptoms include:

    Depression

    Anxiety

    Mood Changes

    Sleep Problems

    Lethargy

    current products for SAD:

    Currently, in-home therapy lights are commonly used as a treatment for those who seek it. An optimal light therapy regimen consists of utilizing light with 2,500 lux intensity for 2 hours daily.

    The light emission has to come from a source not further away than

    61 cm (24 in) from the face of the patient.

     

    For the treatment to be effective, the light must enter the eye of the patient indirectly. A mere skin exposure to light is not sufficient.

    exploration and prototyping

    stroller considerations:

    My team and I chose to put our solution in a public transportation area so that it requires no extra step in the users routine in order to get the benefits. This is important considering that only about a third (35.3%) of those suffering from severe depression seek treatment from a mental health professional. In a station, people can enter in and out freely for light therapy on their way to work. 

    We used Kendall Station as an example.

    We took consideration of, a waiting area, people not being allowed to walk on yellow strip, the space available for the benches and the space needed for people to walk on when they come out of the train. We also considered variable ceiling heights and different uses for the wall behind the benches.

    concepts:

    In our first design exploration, we were thinking that users would openly share how they feel with some sort of interactive mood board that would be placed in the station. In this case, the color of light emitted from the object would be from a gradient scale. As people input their moods by pressing on an emoji the resonate with in that moment, the mean is calculated and so a color from a respective gradient scale would be chosen to display.

    Here, we took away the emojis because a mood is not necessarily representative of overall wellness and also some user testers felt pressured to choose what they thought would be appropriate to have displayed. In this design, users still interact but by choosing colors on a physical interface, to see that in the area that they are waiting in. Though it gives the users the power to change their environment, it wasnt strategic enough in combating SAD.

    In this design exploration, the user still interacts with space since their presence is needed for it to turn on. Their atmosphere still changes positively, but here we have the opportunity to install lighting that is found in therapy lights since the user is in closer proximity to the location of the lighting.

    We decided to explore this idea further for our final design.

    making of LightLounge

    With LightLounge, our final direction, a city can actively counteract somber mood changes due to weather and sunlight deficiency through the use of light in a public high traffic area.

    Mental health is often neglected, and a caring city is one that desire happiness for its citizens.

    We made a working prototype to test how the lights would turn on with the tap of a card, and how if it could turn off due to proximity using an RFID card and reader with LED lights.
